Wilderness Teams

An official small-group party system for the Wilderness — with anti-friendly-fire, a recruitment board, and enforced trust between teammates.

What Is It?
The simplest possible summary
One sentence Wilderness Teams let up to 6 players officially party together in the Wilderness — with anti-friendly-fire, a recruitment board, and a proximity anchor system that keeps teams honest.

The Wilderness has always been built around solo players and large clans. There is almost nothing in between. Wilderness Teams fills that gap — giving small groups of 2 to 6 players a structured way to engage with Wilderness content together without needing to be part of a clan or already knowing the people they're playing with.

The core principle is enforced trust. The anti-friendly-fire rule means your teammates cannot betray you in combat — removing the single biggest reason players refuse to team with strangers in the Wilderness.

  • 6 corresponds to the combat triangle x2 — two of each combat style, which is a natural ceiling for a balanced small group.
  • If unlimited, clans would abuse it to prevent friendly fire en-masse — or players could make a "safe Wilderness" by putting everyone on the same world in one mega-team.
  • 6 players is roughly the upper limit a solo player can realistically escape from — keeping individual encounter stakes meaningful.
  • It encourages intelligent team composition — will you bring 6 PvMers? 6 PKers? A mix? A lunars support with 2 ancients? Every choice matters.
  • The 5-player variant (independently proposed by Danil445) is also viable — Wilderness boss drops split between 10 players, making two teams of 5 a clean fit.
Key Rules
How it actually works
  • Team members cannot attack each other — including multi-target attacks like Ancient Magicks. Full anti-friendly-fire.
  • Teams have a dedicated team chat separate from friends chat and clan chat.
  • A recruitment board near Edgeville and other popular locations lets players advertise their team — similar to the POH advertisement board. It advertises across all worlds and includes a quick-hop button.
  • You can only manually leave or be kicked from a team while in a safe zone — preventing 5 players from luring a 6th and un-teaming mid-Wilderness for a kill.
  • Proximity Rule: The Team Leader is the "Anchor Player". You are automatically removed from the team if you go farther than 1.5x a render distance from the Anchor. If the Anchor dies, the next player in the list becomes the Anchor.
  • At stairs, ladders, dungeon entrances, and teleport levers, the proximity distance is counted from the point of entry — and ignores vertical elevation.
  • Teams are capped at 6 players. Any player wanting a larger group should join a clan instead.
Most of the ingredients already exist in the game. POH advertisement boards. Party chat systems. Player glows. Friendly-fire prevention already exists in PvP minigames. Attack option adjustments are already in your settings. Wilderness Teams is largely assembling existing systems into one coherent feature.
The Luring Problem — Solved
Why anti-friendly-fire is the critical ingredient

Without anti-friendly-fire, Wilderness Teams would be a luring tool. Here is the scam that would otherwise be possible:

❌ Without Anti-Friendly-Fire
A player forms a team of 3 and recruits a wealthy stranger. Separately, 6 of their friends form another team and "organically" encounter the group in the Wilderness. The friends then kill the wealthy stranger while the original team pretends to fight back. The stranger loses their bank. Their teammates walk away clean.
✔ With Anti-Friendly-Fire
This scam cannot happen because your teammates physically cannot deal damage to you. The anti-friendly-fire rule is what transforms Wilderness Teams from a luring vector into a genuine trust system. Strangers can now team up with confidence.
Who Does This Help?
Benefits by player type
🧑‍🤝‍🧑 The Solo PvMer

Can now team up with complete strangers safely. Being 1 of 6 targets reduces your individual chance of being singled out. You are no longer alone in a hostile environment.

The PKer

More players in the Wilderness means more activity. Small PKing teams finally have an official system. There used to be singles teams before the PJ timer change — this brings that back.

🆕 The New Player

Can team up with more experienced players who are incentivised to teach them — because a better-performing teammate helps the whole team. Real apprenticeship through actual gameplay.

🏰 The Clan

Clans can advertise through the recruitment board. Clan members can sub-divide into teams of 6 and benefit from anti-friendly-fire within their sub-teams. A great recruitment pipeline for new members.

👥 Friends Groups

Already have 2-6 friends you want to PK or boss with? This system is perfect for you exactly as described. Anti-friendly-fire just makes it official and reliable.

⚙ Jagex

Turns toxic clan territory behaviour into legitimate gameplay with rules. Gives Jagex a social infrastructure to build future Wilderness content around. Mostly uses existing systems — lower dev cost than a new boss or area.

The Core Design Insight
What Wilderness Teams is actually doing

If you are alone in the Wilderness, you are targeted 100% of the time when a PKer finds you. If you have one teammate, that drops to 50%. With 5 teammates it is ~17%.

Wilderness Teams reduces individual risk not by changing the rules of engagement — but by dividing it across more players. The Wilderness becomes less hostile not because it gets safer, but because you are no longer the only target.

This same principle applies to any Wilderness content designed to reward cooperative play. If a Wilderness boss rewarded better GP per hour for groups, players would naturally gravitate toward forming impromptu alliances — even with strangers. Wilderness Teams provides the social infrastructure to make that happen consistently rather than accidentally.

Goal: PKers should become a reason players get into PvP rather than a reason players stay out of it. Wilderness Teams incentivises PKers to teach new players — because a better teammate directly benefits the PKer they are playing alongside.
