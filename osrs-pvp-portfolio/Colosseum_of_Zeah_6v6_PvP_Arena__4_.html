Colosseum of Zeah

6v6 PvP Arena Minigame

Player-Made Design Document — Not Official — @Chdata

The Colosseum of Zeah is a structured team PvP minigame based in Civitas illa Fortis. Teams of 6 face off in no-holds-barred combat for ELO ranking, seasonal prestige, and access to a unique reward shop.

Supplies are provided by the minigame — outside food, potions, and equipment are standardised or prohibited inside the arena. What you bring is your gear; what the arena provides is the rest.

TL;DR: Ranked 6v6 PvP with ELO matchmaking, combat level brackets, Casual and Competitive modes, and a tiered reward shop gated by your rank. Active on 1-2 dedicated worlds to prevent boosting.
Combat Formats
6v6 is the primary focus. Everything else is secondary.
6v6
Main Format

The core experience. Enough players for mixed strategy — 2 mages, 2 rangers, 2 warriors, or all 6 bridders. Matches last a satisfying length without dragging on.

3v3
Maybe later

Possible future addition if the playerbase grows large enough to sustain it without splitting queues.

1v1
Duel Arena exists

The Duel Arena and Fist of Guthix cover this. Best left out at launch.

Why 6? The combat triangle has 3 styles — and 6 players per team (multiples of 3) feels natural for Runescape. It also mirrors formats that work well in games like Team Fortress 2 and Overwatch. Splitting formats too early splits the playerbase and empties every queue.
Game Modes
One for fun. One for glory.
🛡️
Casual Colosseum

No ELO, no Valiance Factor. An invisible MMR still prevents brand new players from fighting maxed veterans. Good for practice, learning team compositions, and friendly duels between pre-made parties.

🏆
Competitive Colosseum

ELO ranking and Valiance Factor rewards. Wins earn your team rating; losses cost it. Rating fluctuates across seasons. Top-ranked teams at season end earn a shared GP prize pool.

Rewards go in Competitive. Attaching Valiance Factor to Competitive encourages people to actually try — because if you can farm rewards without trying, the competitive spirit dies fast.
Matchmaking & ELO Ranking
How you find a fight. How fights stay fair.

The Colosseum is only active on 1-2 dedicated worlds (one US, one UK). Once your party of 6 is assembled, the Team Leader queues for a match. The system finds an opponent team of similar ELO and the same combat level bracket — both filters apply simultaneously.

Combat Level Brackets

ELO alone can't protect a level 80 account from being thrown against a level 126 main. Brackets handle this — like Pest Control divisions, but for PvP. Each bracket has its own separate ELO ladder.

Intermediate
CB 75-100
Veteran
CB 101-126

This opens the minigame to PKing account builds — zerkers, pure pkers, void builds — not just mains. An unbracketed free-for-all mode may also be offered for players who want it.

ELO Tiers

Your team's ELO rating starts at 1000. Every win earns rating; every loss costs it. Rating earned scales with opponent strength — stomping a lower-rated team gains less than beating one at your level.

Seasons

Similar to Deadman Mode, the Colosseum runs in competitive seasons of 1 or 3 months. When a season ends, rankings reset — keeping the meta fresh as new gear enters the game. Past season data is preserved, so you can look back years later at how you ranked.

ELO caveats: ELO systems are never perfect at launch. Expect some lopsided matches early on. The system doesn't have to be perfect to be fun — just directionally correct. Even Overwatch's matchmaking was rough at first.
Inside the Arena
What the game gives you. What you bring yourself.
Provided by the Minigame
  • Colosseum Bandages — restores HP and run energy, but does not cure poison or venom. Encourages Lunar Magic utility spells.
  • Colosseum Potions — likely a prayer pot or two. Makes Share Potion spells via Lunars worth using.
  • Free Catalytic Runes — supplied to all players. Removes the barrier to casting Lunar group spells — Cure Poison, Heal Other, Group Vengeance — that are otherwise never brought to PvP.
Lunar Magic Inside the Colosseum

Because catalytic runes are free, players will actually use Lunar spells they normally never bother with. Group Vengeance, Heal Other, Cure Venom — these spells finally have a context where bringing the runes makes sense.

Why this matters: Colosseum is one of the few places in the game that could genuinely encourage cooperative spellcasting. A dedicated Lunar support role could be viable here.
Valiance Factor & Rewards
Currency earned through Competitive wins. Spent in tiered shops.

Valiance Factor (VF) is the minigame currency. Higher-rated players earn more VF per win. The shop is split into tiers locked behind your ELO rank — lower-tier shops stay accessible even if your rating drops later. You never lose access to what you've already unlocked.

Most consumable rewards (runes, ammo) aren't intended to be good gp/h. You're meant to spend VF on the unique rewards. The filler items exist to make the shop feel complete and to give you somewhere to spend overflow points.

Reward structure philosophy: Untradeable rewards (armour, gloves, robes) are concentrated in the lower tiers and unranked shop - they're gated by rating, not by being sellable. Tradeable rewards with real monetary value (runes, ammo, charging dust) are pushed into higher-ranked shops, making them harder for bots to reach. A bot that can't win matches can't earn money here.
Pricing note: VF costs aren't specified here - Jagex would design those. Item costs could be static, or tied to G.E. prices and fluctuate dynamically. Dynamic pricing makes more sense: it lets the rewards affect the economy rather than being wrecked by it.
All Ranked Shops
Available to every ranked player, Bronze through Diamond.
  • Rank Hood/Hat & Rank Cape — Cosmetic items that display your current tier. Cheap or free. The cape has an Operate function that Quick-Chats the date and competitive season in which you first obtained it.
  • Prestige Pets — Gold, Platinum, and Diamond ranks each have a unique pet available, for players who want to show off their rating that way.
Unranked Shop
Available even at 0 or negative rating.
  • Minigame Hybrid Armour Pieces — Level 75 armour designed for switching. Intentionally not overpowered. Available here for various design reasons — primarily to reduce the gear gap for newer players entering the minigame.
  • Recharge Colosseum Gloves — Costs either 100% VF, or 50% VF + 500k gp, or 1m gp outright. VF and GP options are intended to be roughly equal in value here, unlike the robes.
  • Recharge Catalytic Robes — Costs either 100% VF, or 50% VF + 250k gp, or 500k gp. Recharging back to 100% costs roughly 10% of the original robe price in VF.
  • Recharge Elemental Robes — Costs either 100% VF, or 50% VF + 50k gp, or 100k gp. The VF option is intentionally much cheaper than the GP option here — robes are a bigger grind.
  • Season History Display (POH) — A Player-Owned House item that records your ELO rating and win count for each past competitive season.

Gold Shop
3000+ Rating
  • Colosseum Gloves — The best reward. Starts at 25% charge when bought; 100% charge lasts 8 hours in combat. Untradeable while charged. Available in black or white. Must be recharged via VF or GP when depleted. Three variants: Feint Gloves (melee), Sleight Gloves (ranged), Meister Gloves (magic).
  • Glove Enchantment Token — Tradeable alternative for players who don't want to engage in PvP. Transforms a set of Barrows Gloves into one Colosseum Glove variant of your choice. May produce "Corrupted" gloves that crumble to dust when fully degraded and cannot be recharged. Should probably be polled rather than assumed.
  • Catalytic Robes — Based on Fist of Guthix Battle Robes. Requires 50 Magic, 50 Defence. Degrades after 6 hours of combat. Bought at 100% charge; only tradeable while uncharged. Set effect: 20% chance to supply one type of catalytic rune when casting a combat spell; SotD's 12.5% rune saving is increased to 50%.
  • 25x Dreadnips — A callback to the Dominion Tower. OSRS version requires 75 Crafting to unfold. Low power level — more of a fun novelty. Could equally be left out.

Diamond Shop
5000+ Rating — The peak of the reward ladder.
  • Bag of Glove Supercharging Dust — Fully charges Colosseum Gloves to 125% (10 hours instead of 8). Cost equals a standard full recharge — meaning you get more duration for the same price. Not stackable, not tradeable.
  • Bag of Catalytic Supercharging Dust — Extends Catalytic Robes from 6 hours to 7.5 hours (125% charge). Possibly tradeable, not stackable.
  • Dragon Darts — 100x per purchase.
  • Dragon Knives — 100x per purchase.
  • Dragon Thrownaxes — 10x per purchase.
  • Wrath Runes — 50x per purchase. Can only be bought by giving an on-staff Arceuus mage a Wrath Talisman.
  • Life Runes — 50x per purchase. Speculative — only relevant if the Necromancy spellbook receives further development.
All ranged rewards are thrown weapons. This is intentional — thrown weapons tie directly to the Sleight Gloves reward. Most rewards across all tiers are combat-focused because this is a PvP minigame.
Season-End Prize Pool
The #1 team at the end of each season wins real gp.

The top-ranked team at season end wins a shared GP prize pool. Each of the six players receives 32m gp, for a total payout of 192m gp distributed across the roster.

Over a one-month season this averages to roughly 1m gp per day per player on the winning team — meaningful recognition of sustained excellence without being large enough to distort the economy. Three-month seasons would scale accordingly.

Why this works: It's a one-time per-season payout going to exactly one team of six. Not a farmable income stream. It gives top-level competitive play something tangible to fight for beyond cosmetics and prestige.
Season Modifiers
Each competitive season can run under a different ruleset.

Seasons run for 3 months each, giving 4 seasons per year. Every other season uses a different ruleset - normal seasons and modifier seasons alternate. A full year might look like this:

  • Season 1 - Normal rules
  • Season 2 - No switching worn equipment
  • Season 3 - Normal rules
  • Season 4 - Damage dealt regenerates special attack energy

This keeps a normal season as the baseline that players always return to, while giving modifier seasons enough time for teams to actually adapt and develop a meta around the ruleset rather than feeling like a novelty that's over before anyone figures it out.

At launch, Jagex could run monthly seasons initially - to build momentum, keep the format in the news, and gather data on which modifiers land well with players - before settling into the 3-month cadence.

Listed below are a range of modifier ideas Jagex could draw from.

Confirmed Modifier Ideas
  • Bounty Hunter Rules - No protection prayers. Magical freezes have no effect. Combat becomes pure damage trading with no safe prayer tanking and no freeze-locking plays.
  • Emir's Arena Rules - Equalized combat stats. Everyone is treated as maxed. Removes gear and level advantages entirely - pure skill and team coordination only.
  • No Special Attacks - All special attacks are disabled. KO potential is dramatically reduced; fights are decided by sustained damage and attrition rather than spec fishing.
  • LMS Rules - Kills instantly restore the killer's HP and prayer to full. Snowballing is real - getting the first kill gives your team a significant momentum advantage.
  • Last Stand - The last surviving player on each team gains a major power boost: quadruple spec restore rate, infinite prayer, and a one-time 25% overheal. Creates dramatic clutch potential.
  • Infinite Run Energy - Run energy never depletes. Kiting, repositioning, and map control become far more viable as strategies.
  • Melee Only - Only melee combat is permitted inside the arena. Tests pure melee skill and positioning without the prayer-switching and style-switching meta.
  • Magic Only - Only magic combat is permitted. Freeze control, splash rates, and rune management take center stage.
  • Ranged Only - Only ranged combat is permitted. Emphasis on movement, distance management, and ammunition conservation.
  • Singles Teams - The arena switches from multi-combat to single-way combat. Despite being a 6v6, each player can only be attacked by one opponent at a time - no pile-ons. The PJ timer is short, closer to the old Bounty Hunter world standard rather than the extended Wilderness timer. Fundamentally changes team tactics: it becomes six simultaneous 1v1s rather than a team brawl.
  • Fist of Guthix Style - Defence bonuses from stats and equipment are heavily dampened, making armour choice nearly irrelevant. Without the safety net of high defence, weapon switches and prayer switches become the primary way to survive - rewarding players with sharp tribrid mechanics over those who rely on tanky gear setups.
Additional Ideas
  • No Food - Colosseum bandages are removed. Fights end faster and burst damage matters more. Rewards teams that can coordinate KO specs without needing to eat through them.
  • No Potions - Stat-boosting potions are disabled. Levels play at their natural values, which combined with Emir's Arena rules would mean truly equalized play.
  • Spec Energy from Damage - Dealing damage generates special attack energy. Creates a high-offense feedback loop where landing hits rewards you with more offensive tools.
  • No Switching - Worn equipment cannot be changed once the match starts. Rewards pre-planning your gear setup and punishes reliance on style switching as the primary combat mechanic.
  • Vengeance Always Active - Group Vengeance is permanently active on all players with no cooldown. Forces teams to think carefully before dumping damage into a target.
Why this matters for spectating: A Singles Teams season or a No Food season looks completely different on screen from a standard match. Rotating modifiers give streamers and viewers a reason to tune back in each season, and give teams something new to theorycraft and discuss publicly - which drives organic interest in the minigame.
Anti-Abuse Design
Why this is harder to bot and boost than most minigames.
  • No empty-world boosting — limited to 1-2 dedicated worlds only. Can't queue a match if there are no real opponents.
  • ELO decay on boosted wins — repeatedly beating the same team at tanking rating gains little. The system self-corrects.
  • VF scales with rating — players below 500 rating cannot gain Valiance Factor. High-value rewards require high ranking.
  • Bots need real stats and real gear — unlike LMS, which hands everyone free maxed stats and gear, the Colosseum uses your actual account. A bot host would need to build and equip six real accounts with combat stats and valuable gear — all of which are at risk of being lost in matches. That's a meaningful barrier compared to content where the game hands you everything.
  • Colosseum Gloves start at 25% charge — even if bought, they require ongoing VF investment to maintain. Not a one-time grind.
  • Prize pool requires winning the season — the GP reward goes to exactly one team. There is no second-place farming; you either win the season or you don't. Bots can't win a season ladder.
  • Quest point requirements per shop tier — each reward shop tier could require a minimum quest point total to access, scaling from 0 QP for the unranked shop up to around 300 QP for the Platinum shop, with appropriate thresholds for Bronze, Silver, and Gold in between. Quest points are one of the hardest account metrics for bot farms to fake at scale, since quests require varied gameplay that resists automation. A bot farm building six accounts to 300 QP each is a significant investment that raises the cost of entry substantially.
Spectating & Competitive Appeal
It won't be the next esport. But it could be streamable.

A spectator mode is a natural fit — low dev priority compared to core gameplay, but it gives the format streaming potential. 6v6 team fights with mixed combat styles, coordinated specs, and Lunar support plays are inherently more watchable than solo PKing clips.

At the top of the Diamond bracket, players can no longer rely purely on gear advantage. Teams of equally-geared players have to actually strategise — which combination of styles, which player to focus, when to pop Group Vengeance. That's when interesting metas emerge and the format starts generating clips worth watching.

Note: This will not be a perfectly balanced competitive game at launch. Expect some dominant strategies, some overpowered specs, and some weird bracket interactions. That is acceptable. Fun first. Balance second.
