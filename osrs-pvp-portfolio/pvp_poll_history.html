Chdata's Take
Chdata's Analysis
This is my own read on what's happening to PvP polling - pulled from my video script, in my own words. The data analysis right after this is separate and was put together independently; there's a "Note from Claude" at the end of this section on whether my take actually holds up against the numbers.

PvP updates almost never pass Old School's 70% threshold. The easy explanation is "the updates were just bad" or "everyone hates PvP now," but I don't think that's really it. I've got two points, and I'll start with the second one because it's the structural one.

A 70% poll is antithetical to multiplayer PvP balance.

Think of the Wilderness as cats and mice. If you ask the cats "should we break the ankles of every mouse so you can catch them easier," most cats vote yes. The mice, obviously, vote no. Assuming roughly equal populations you land at 50/50 - never a 70% consensus. It's impossible by design. And it doesn't matter which way you slice the voting: let only cats vote and they build a Wilderness the mice flee from; let only mice vote and they hand the cats a chance to trip over their own feet mid-chase; let both vote and you get a permanent deadlock. All three roads lead to the same systematic failure.

Players vote in their own self-interest. So when you make them vote on the one part of the game that pits Player versus Player, they naturally vote against each other. Want to nerf the Voidwaker special? One side benefits, the other side gets hurt. Flip it and buff the Voidwaker - now the other side is hurt instead. Almost anything in PvP has someone on the offensive end and someone on the receiving end, so getting both of them to a 70% agreement... the math is just not mathing. Compare that to a PvM or skilling update, which basically gives everyone new toys and takes nothing away from anyone. Like, who's voting no on a PvM update? The monsters?

And here's the kicker - Old School is something like 90% non-PvPers. When that 90% can vote on PvP balance, they decide PvP's fate. The real-world result is that PvP has been shut down by the community itself. It's a polling doom spiral: Jagex waits for organic demand to climb to 70%, but that gets less likely every single year as PKers quit and new players and ironmen pour in. Speaking of which - if ironmen are something like 30% of the playerbase and they've got zero reason to vote yes on PvP, that's a 30% bloc of automatic no votes before you even start. At some point Jagex has to decide whether PvP is worth investing in on its own merits, or just let the current system run its slow death sentence.

Now, I'm not actually saying "scrap the polls." The community-driven nature of polling is the true spirit of Old School, and polls keep Jagex accountable for bad updates - Wrathmaw and the Calamity armour really did have flaws. I just think PvP is the one place where the 70% model breaks down, and that's worth being honest about.

The failures are telling us we keep proposing the wrong kind of thing.

That's my first point, circled back to. For 10+ years Jagex has reached for the same category of solution - new loot-pinata content, new rewards, new bosses - without touching the actual core problems: risk not being worth the reward, the skill gap, and the lack of good activities built for different skill levels. So trying more of the same isn't what we need and isn't what players are asking for. I think the constant failure of PvP polls is the community telling us these aren't the solutions - not that PvP is hopeless.

I also think limiting polls to "only PKers" is backwards. That's textbook survivorship bias - the PKers are the survivors who already like the Wilderness, so asking only them why it's dead points Jagex at the wrong things. (For the record: by the PvP Arena poll's own rules I'm "not a PvPer" because I don't have an LMS win or 20 Wildy kills, and I wasn't allowed into the official PvP feedback Discord I applied to. I love PvP. I just like a different kind of it.) Wilderness gameplay concerns the whole community, so the solution has to be pollable to the whole community.

And to be clear - not every PvP poll fails, people forget that. Bounty Hunter changes passed. Wildy Agility passed. LMS, Castle Wars, and Soul Wars reward changes passed. It just feels like everything fails because the big, contested stuff fails, or gets rolled back later. It really does come down to finding the right designs.

My fix: PvP Update Ranking Polls.

Instead of binary yes/no, Jagex shows ~10 PvP proposals and we rank them 1-10. Whatever's #1 gets built. The other nine aren't "ruled out" - they come back in the next ranking poll. This sidesteps the whole cat-and-mouse problem because nothing is being denied; it just turns the competitiveness toward deciding what matters most. Half the time I vote no on something not because I hate it, but because I'd rather the dev time went elsewhere, or because the idea needs another pass - and a yes/no/skip vote can't say any of that. (I'd also add a "Needs Improvement" option to lock-in polls.) It's more honest than the current setup where Jagex has to guess what we want and then watch it die at poll.

I'm not pulling this out of nowhere - I used to run Team Fortress 2 servers and make my own balance changes for my own game mode, and I saw the exact same pattern. Every change had a side that loved it and a side that hated it, and there was almost never a consensus. Direct feedback alone was useless because every complaint had a defender. What actually helped was data: usage rates, win rates, and - the big one - satisfaction surveys. Because "people use this a lot" is a completely different thing from "people like using this," which is a completely different thing again from "people like having to fight against this." Old School is the most community-driven game on the planet, and I think Jagex should track and publish a lot more of that kind of PvP data instead of leaving it a guessing game.

Note from Claude

Does the take fit the data? Mostly yes, and the strongest part is the strongest claim. The "cat and mouse" argument - that zero-sum PvP balance can't reach 70% because it pits players against each other - shows up cleanly in the voting record. Every one of the 13 questions that landed in the 45-55% dead-heat band is a balance change; not a single one is content. The exact risk-shifting changes the argument predicts would split the room did split it: Protect Item on PvP worlds (42.8%), making dropped food/potions invisible (48.5%), instant drop visibility (51.8%), the multi-combat rev zone (50.6%). And all nine "should this PvP reward also work against NPCs / outside PvP" questions failed - the PvM majority vetoing PvP power leaking into PvE is the cat-and-mouse dynamic in miniature.

The "we keep proposing the wrong thing" point also holds up, maybe more than the script claims. The accessibility content aimed straight at the skill-gap problem actually passed comfortably - the LMS minigame at 87.6% and the PvP Tutorial at 83.1%. So the lever Chdata points to (positive-sum content that helps everyone rather than redistributing risk) is exactly what the data says clears the bar. The deprioritization worry has a real signature too: contested balance questions fell from 33% of the PvP slate before Oct 2022 to 13% after, with the hardest ones quietly routed to unpolled integrity updates instead.

Where I'd add precision: "PvP almost never passes" is a touch overstated as written. Across the whole record it's 60.7%, and content-type questions pass 73% - it's balance changes that fail (33%), not PvP wholesale. Chdata's own "not all PvP polls fail" caveat is the more accurate framing, and it's worth leading with because it's harder to dismiss. The ironman-30%-bloc mechanism is plausible and consistent with the pattern, but this dataset can't directly measure who voted which way, so it should be presented as a reasonable inference rather than a proven number. Net: the core thesis - that a 70% consensus is structurally hostile to zero-sum PvP balance, and the way out is positive-sum design plus a ranking system - is well-supported. The one edit I'd make is to say "balance changes fail," not "PvP fails."

AI Data Analysis
What the Numbers Actually Say
This section analyses the 178 scored yes/no questions above (the single multiple-choice question is excluded from rates). Every figure is computed directly from the data on this page. A question "passed" if its Yes% met the threshold that applied on its date - 75% before 27 Oct 2022, 70% after. Where a claim is a genuine causal story it is labelled; where it is only a correlation - or a time artifact dressed up as one - that is said plainly, because a portfolio that overclaims is easy to shoot down.
⚠ A note on the numbers

Unlike the community sentiment page, these figures are hard data. Every vote percentage, total, and pass/fail result is pulled directly from the official Old School RuneScape poll results - real recorded outcomes, not estimates.

What's interpretive is the sorting, not the counting. The Balance / Content / Other categories are defined by Chdata; and then Claude AI interpreted each question and sorted it into the most fitting category. A few could reasonably be filed differently, which nudges the grouped breakdowns slightly but never the underlying pass rates.

The deductions and analysis are softer still: one reasonable reading of the record, not the only one. But the core numbers are solid, and the headline patterns hold no matter how you slice them.

The Single Biggest Finding
PvP polls did not gradually get harder or easier to pass. The headline jump - from a 44% pass rate (51/116) under the old 75% threshold to 92% (57/62) after the charter dropped it to 70% on 27 Oct 2022 - has two drivers, and the second one matters more than it first appears.

The first is mechanical: the lower bar plus a rise in average Yes% (71.5% to 80.4%) lifts the rate directly. The second is selection. The threshold change came right after the PvP Arena poll, where 13 contested balance questions failed in a single sheet. Chdata's deduction: that failure marks the point where Jagex grew hesitant to put controversial PvP changes to a poll at all. The data fits this. Balance questions fell from 33% of the PvP slate before Oct 2022 to just 13% after, and 2023, 2025 and 2026 polled zero of them. The balance questions that did survive to a vote post-2022 were the safe ones - Soul Wars safespotting fixes and Castle Wars restorations at the community's own request - which all passed at 82%+. The genuinely divisive zero-sum changes were not being approved by a more agreeable electorate; they were no longer on the ballot. They moved instead to the unpolled / integrity channel (see the Unpolled section).

This is why the "self-defeating polls" dynamic is better described as persistent than as a one-year spike. Whenever a contested balance change was actually polled - 2013, 2014, 2016, 2017, 2021, 2022 - it tended to fail, across the entire timeline. The post-2022 pass rate is partly a survivorship statistic: it looks like the problem was solved, when in practice the questions that would have failed simply stopped being asked. The cleanest takeaway still holds, and gets sharper under this lens: the type of change predicts the outcome far better than the year does - and after 2022 the hardest types were quietly routed around the poll entirely.

The Identifiable Poll "Types" (by content)
Sorting the questions by what they actually propose - rather than the three coarse Balance/Content/Other buckets - six recurring types fall out. Ranked best to worst by how reliably they pass:
1. New content & minigamesBest
LMS, Soul Wars, Castle Wars additions; Wilderness resources, bosses, teleports. The backbone of the "content" bucket. Reliable passers as long as the design is solid - this is where almost every modern PvP win lives.
2. "Restore old behaviour" requestsStrong
Castle Wars 2008 terrain/spears, PID back toward 2007 consistency. Framing a change as restoring a nostalgic, community-requested behaviour is a strong tailwind - these clear threshold comfortably.
3. Clear anti-grief / QoL mechanicsStrong when obvious
BH target-swap delay (91%), visible Teleblock animation (92%), K/D tracking. When a QoL fix is unambiguously good for everyone it scores in the high 80s-90s. The catch: if the "QoL" change quietly shifts risk or reward, it drops into the contested zone instead.
4. Reward-shop itemsCoin-toss
LMS / Soul Wars / PvP Arena store unlocks. Splits cleanly by what the item does: cosmetics, capes and halos pass (75-82%); items that grant combat power - especially power that leaks outside the minigame - sink toward 60% and below.
5. PvP power usable in PvM ("can it work against NPCs?")Reliably fails
The 2022 PvP Arena armour set produced eight near-identical "should this also be usable against NPCs / outside PvP" sub-questions that all landed at 53-54% and failed. The PvM-majority electorate vetoes letting PvP grind shortcut into PvE power. This is the single most predictable failure pattern on the page.
6. Zero-sum combat / interaction rulesWorst
Drop visibility, singles vs multi, Protect Item on/off, attack-while-in-NPC-combat, KBD tele-lock. These directly move risk from one camp to the other and stall near 50/50. Tellingly, several were eventually shipped by Jagex as unpolled integrity changes because they could never clear a poll - see the Unpolled section above.

Trends Worth Checking That You Didn't List
The dev slate mirrors the playerbase: ~90% non-PvP on both sides
PvP/Wilderness questions are just 178 of 1,682 total poll questions - 10.6%, so close to 90% of everything polled is non-PvP. That lines up almost exactly with the commonly-cited estimate that roughly 90% of the playerbase doesn't engage with PvP. The two 90%s reinforce each other: Jagex allocates development and polling roughly in proportion to demonstrated demand, so the share of PvP updates tracks the share of PvP players. Caveat worth stating out loud - the 89% is measured directly here, but the 90%-non-PvP-population figure is a widely-repeated estimate, not something this voting record can prove. The honest framing is that the update mix is consistent with the population estimate, which is exactly the "invest in proportion to current demand" loop the proposals are trying to break.
The "74% graveyard"
19 questions failed at 70-74.9% under the old bar - i.e. they had majority-plus support and died only because 75% was unreachable. This is the most concrete evidence the charter change mattered, and a ready-made argument: a big slice of "rejected" PvP content was never actually unpopular.
Batching correlates results - it does not rescue them
When Jagex split one contested idea into many sub-questions (the eight ~53% PvP Arena armour items), they all failed together. When they batched popular content (Poll 83's 13 Castle Wars questions), they nearly all passed together. Questions inside one poll move as a bloc, so framing and blog reception lift or sink the whole sheet. Splitting a divisive concept into pieces just manufactures many correlated failures.
Raw vote counts are not comparable across years
Median turnout grew ~6x (12.7k in 2013 to 77.6k in 2026) with the playerbase. Any cross-year analysis must use Yes% or skip rate, never absolute votes or skips - otherwise you are mostly measuring how many people were online that year.
There is a hard approval ceiling around 90%
Almost nothing exceeds 94%, even universally loved QoL. The realistic target for a proposal is to clear threshold with margin, not to chase unanimity - so a design landing in the low-to-mid 80s is already a strong result, not a lukewarm one.
The distribution is bimodal, and the danger zone is 70-79%
Questions cluster at "clearly good" (80s) or "contested" (50s-60s), with a thin true middle. The 70-79% band is where both thresholds live and where outcomes are most fragile - a proposal here is exactly the kind worth re-framing toward positive-sum content before it ever reaches a poll.
"Failed a poll" is not the end of the road
Several zero-sum balance changes that could not pass were later shipped as unpolled integrity updates (instant drop visibility failed twice at 51-57%, then went in anyway; Rev Caves multi-to-singles). The polling record understates how much contested change actually happens - it just happens through a different door. A proposal framed as integrity rather than as a vote-bait balance question follows a completely different, and often more permissive, path.
How the Voting Record Maps to the Core Problems
The landing page lists the core problems facing PvP. Three of them are claims about polling and outcomes, so the voting record can be checked directly against them. Here is what the data does and doesn't support.
Problem 1 - Risk is not worth the reward
Supported, with an important twist. The poll record shows the community will not vote to redistribute risk between the two camps: every change that moved risk one way or the other stalled near 50/50 - Protect Item on PvP worlds (42.8%), instant drop visibility (51.8%), making dropped food/potions invisible (48.5%). That means the risk-vs-reward problem is essentially unsolvable through a balance poll; it has to be addressed with positive-sum content that adds reward without taking anything from anyone (which is exactly the design space Blessed Status aims at). The polls don't say players are happy with the risk - they say they won't approve a zero-sum fix for it.
Problem 2 - The skill gap is too large
Polls are not the blocker here - design is. The accessibility content aimed squarely at the skill gap actually passed comfortably: the LMS minigame at 87.6% and the PvP Tutorial at 83.1%. So the community will vote yes on skill-gap solutions when they are framed as new, opt-in content for everyone. The reason the skill gap persists isn't poll rejection; it's that the things that passed (LMS, PvP Arena) didn't fully deliver the teaching ladder in practice. This is a build-quality problem, not a polling problem - which is good news for any well-designed accessibility proposal.
Problem 3 - The self-defeating PvP polls
This is the one the data backs most directly. The "90% non-PvPers vote on PvP balance and it can't reach 70%" claim is visible in the numbers: balance changes pass 33% vs content's 73%, and all 13 questions in the 45-55% dead-heat band are balance, zero are content. The "us vs them / it can only please ~50%" point is literally the shape of the data - a fair 50/50 split is the signature of a contested balance question. And the deprioritization loop shows up as the post-2022 collapse in contested balance polling (33% of the slate down to 13%), with the hardest changes rerouted to unpolled integrity updates. The ironman-bloc mechanism is consistent with all of this but can't be measured from vote totals alone, so treat it as a strong inference rather than a proven figure.
Predator-prey design & the integrity escape hatch
Backed, and it points at the proposed fix. The "force PvP through as integrity updates" path that the script weighs is already how several contested changes actually shipped: instant drop visibility failed at the polls twice (51-57%) and went in anyway; the Rev Caves multi-to-singles overhaul was an unpolled integrity change. So the binary poll genuinely is a dead end for zero-sum balance - which is the practical case for a ranking-style system that lets positive-sum PvP content compete on priority rather than survival. The "usable against NPCs" pattern (9 of 9 failed) is the clearest single demonstration that the PvM majority will veto anything that lets PvP power leak into PvE, exactly as the cat-and-mouse framing predicts.

Community Sentiment vs. The Poll Record
This section holds the Community Sentiment Analysis - roughly 7,000 comments, posts and replies across 13 YouTube, Twitter/X and Reddit sources - up against the 178-question voting record above. The question is simple: when players say "PvP polls are broken" or "nobody will ever approve this," does the actual ballot back them up? Mostly it does. And where the two disagree, it's usually because the comment sections are more pessimistic than the votes actually are.
The Headline
The community's single most poll-relevant grievance - "PvP updates fail the 70% threshold because the non-PvP majority votes no" - is the 4th most-mentioned problem in the sentiment data and the highest-engagement one on Reddit by a wide margin (210,302 upvotes, 32,417 comments across the threads). It is also the grievance the voting record confirms most cleanly: balance changes pass 33% vs content's 73%, and every one of the 13 dead-heat (45-55%) questions is a balance change. The players diagnosed the exact failure mode the data shows. The one correction the ballot makes to the mood is hopeful: the things commenters assume will fail often don't.

The One Place They Diverge
The sentiment model and the voting record agree on what fails - zero-sum balance, forced-PvP rewards, anything that picks a side. They part ways on what passes. The comment sections, soaked in "PvP is dead" fatalism, lowball positive-sum content: the PvP Tutorial was estimated around 66% and actually passed at 83.1%. The lesson for the video is that the pessimism is calibrated to the wrong category. It's accurate about contested balance and wrong about broadly-useful content - which is precisely the kind of content the four proposals are built to be. The sentiment read of "they'll vote it down" is a warning about how you frame a change, not a verdict on whether good PvP content can pass.
What Jagex Polls vs. What the Community Is Loudest About
Classify the 178 questions Jagex actually polled by what they do, and a lopsided diet appears: roughly 107 add a reward or item, 73 add new content (bosses, minigames, zones), about 19 touch death or risk mechanics, and a grand total of 4 touch bots, cheating or enforcement. Now line that up against what the community is loudest about. The five highest-engagement grievances by Reddit upvotes are the broken poll system (210K), predator-prey toxicity (148K), the us-vs-them culture war (121K), bots and AHK cheating (111K), and forced-Wilderness rewards (101K). Almost none of those are things Jagex puts to a poll.

This is the clearest data-backed version of the "Jagex acknowledges it but doesn't act on it" suspicion. It isn't that Jagex ignores the feedback - JMods reference these exact problems on streams and in blogs. It's that the poll is a tool for shipping additive, positive-sum content, and the community's biggest pain points are structural, zero-sum, or cultural - the precise categories a 70% binary poll can't carry. So the things that get polled are new bosses and reward shops, while the things that get talked about most get a blog acknowledgment and an unpolled integrity update at best. The poll diet reflects what can pass, not what the community most wants fixed.
Risk vs. Reward: Which Lever Actually Gets Pulled
"Too much risk" and "not enough reward" isn't a side-theme in the community data - it's the dominant one. Across the full 40-problem list it threads through roughly 25 of 40 problems and about 57% of all problem engagement (mentions and Reddit upvotes alike). And the community's framing leans hard toward the risk side: asymmetric risk with no minimum floor for PKers (#8), no way to learn without real financial loss (#16), clue steps that cost time and gear (#2), ironmen losing hard-earned items (#3), ragging (#25), wasted-trip frustration (#31). The recurring message is "the risk is too punishing," not "the loot isn't shiny enough."
Now look at which lever the poll record shows Jagex pulling. Of the two ways to fix a risk/reward imbalance - add reward or reduce risk - Jagex polls the reward lever about 5 times as often (~107 reward/item additions vs ~19 risk-mechanic questions), and the reward lever is the one that passes: new weapons and loot like the Webweaver Bow, Voidwaker, Accursed Sceptre and Skull Sceptre buffs all cleared 84-94%. The risk-reduction lever, when actually pulled, mostly snaps: Protect Item on PvP worlds failed at 42.8%, instant drop visibility at 51.8%, hiding dropped food and potions at 48.5%. The only risk-side wins were narrow, additive QoL tweaks (keeping untradeables on death at 81%, Teleblock removed in safe zones at 82%) - never the structural "cap the downside" change the community is actually asking for.
The mismatch in one line: the community overwhelmingly frames the problem as "too much risk," but the only risk/reward fix that survives a 70% poll is "add more reward" - because reducing risk is a zero-sum balance change, and those die at the ballot. That is exactly the gap Blessed Status is engineered to slip through: an opt-in, fee-paying, PKer-still-gets-paid way to cap the downside without being a zero-sum nerf - a risk-reduction dressed as positive-sum content, because the poll record says that is the only kind that can pass.

One more alignment worth flagging: the sentiment analysis puts the engagement-weighted split among people with a readable stance at roughly 41% pro-PvP to 59% anti-PvP. That is strikingly close to where contested balance questions actually land on the ballot - a hair below the midpoint, in the 45-55% band. The comments and the votes are describing the same electorate from two different angles, and they arrive at nearly the same number. The behavior axis sharpens it further: only about 15% of the conversation actively plays PvP while another ~13% is pro-PvP but sidelined, which is the untapped demand the proposals target - and it explains why a binary poll, dominated by the people already in the Wilderness or already against it, can't represent the players who would show up for a better-designed version.
