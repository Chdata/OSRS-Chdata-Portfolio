Player Mindset Archetypes
Ten stance-bearing mindsets plus a no-position slice, visible across ~7,000 data points · updated across 13 sources · engagement-weighted, normalized to 100%

The comment sections are not a monolith. Ten distinct stance-bearing mindsets shape the conversation — plus an eleventh slice for people who engaged but took no readable position — each with very different relationships to PvP and what they want from it. Sources 2 and 3 (Framed's tutorial video and the Oda react) skewed toward existing PvP-adjacent players. Source 5 (TheoryWiser podcast) drew a more analytical audience — longer, more reasoned comments — and surfaced the sharpest articulations of structural problems like the skill-ceiling death spiral, the parasitic predator-prey dynamic, and why polling will never fix this without first changing who is in the Wilderness and why.

How the percentages are calculated

These shares are an estimate, not a precise count, and they're built one specific way: the denominator is all ~7,000 data points across the 13 sources, and the figures are engagement-weighted — a comment, post, or grievance is counted in proportion to the upvotes and likes it drew, so popular sentiment carries more weight than a single low-traction comment. A commenter who fits two mindsets (e.g. a PvMer who is also a Reluctant Visitor) has their weight split across both rather than double-counted, and the eleven slices are then normalized to sum to exactly 100%, so this reads as a clean pie rather than overlapping tags. The off-topic / meme / no-stance mass isn't discarded — it becomes its own slice ("Commented But Took No Position"), since those people did engage with PvP topics even though no position can be read from them. One important caveat: because this is engagement-weighted, it deliberately differs from a raw headcount. The Reluctant Visitor tops the chart not because it's the largest group of distinct people, but because the highest-engagement threads in the data ("I got ganked doing my clue," "the wildy just wastes my time," "forced into content I don't want") are written by and resonate with people who do go to the Wilderness and resent it. A simple show-of-hands count would likely rank the Anti-PvP Bloc higher. Treat these as the shape of the conversation's energy, not a census of the playerbase.

💀 The Anti-PvP Bloc~16%

The largest hard-no voting bloc, and by raw headcount likely the single biggest stance group — though once weighted by engagement it sits behind the Reluctant Visitor (see the calculation note). They don't want to engage with PvP at all and will vote no on most PvP updates. Source 5 gave this group their most articulate voice yet — several comments were not spite-driven but argued from first principles that the predator-prey model is structurally unfixable within the current polling system, that "juiced drop tables" just create better loot piñatas, and that the 90% of players with no interest in PvP are not the problem — they are correctly reflecting their rational self-interest. Hard to dismiss these as ignorant votes. Core sentiment: "I will always take the side of the person toiling against the person who takes the path of least resistance."

🏃 The Reluctant Visitor~22%

Goes into the Wilderness only when forced — clue steps, MA2 capes, Voidwaker. Actively resents being there. Brings minimum risk. Frequently griefed. This archetype was most prominent in source 3 (Still Framed's wilderness video) and almost entirely absent from sources 1-2, which drew more PvP-curious audiences. Will vote yes for anything that reduces forced interaction.

🔰 The Willing But Helpless~11%

Players who want to PvP but find the skill gap, cost, and cheater environment completely overwhelming. The single most vocal archetype in sources 1 and 2. "I would do it if I had a chance." Source 5 (TheoryWiser) produced the sharpest articulation of this group's problem: "Imagine if you wanted to complete the Inferno but there was nothing to properly train you for it beyond Fight Caves. That's how PvP is right now." Largest potential conversion target. Also the group most likely to cite AHK/client cheating as the specific reason they gave up trying.

⚔️ The PvM Enjoyer~14%

Does Wilderness PvM — agility, chaos altar, revs, bosses — and has made peace with occasional PKing as part of the deal. Generally supports making the Wilderness healthier but doesn't want radical changes. Ironmen who've grinded the Voidwaker fall here. They care more about the predator-prey ratio and cheating environment than about PvP design per se.

🤠 The "Indiana Jones"~2%

The player who enjoys being the prey — for whom the predator-prey dynamic is the whole appeal, a form of hunt-and-escape gameplay rather than something to endure. Crucially distinct from the PvM Enjoyer, who tolerates PKing as a tax on the content they actually want: the Indiana Jones wants the chase, and would lose something real if it were removed. This mindset is almost never quoted directly in the comment data, which is exactly the point — it's a playstyle that has likely been all but lost. It used to have homes: capturing the flag in Castle Wars puts you in the prey role, and pre-OSRS Fist of Guthix was an entire predator-vs-prey PvP minigame built around it. These are the players who are positive about predator-prey design in the Wilderness, if only it were designed properly, and the people asking for better "escape and chase" mechanics are likely sympathetic to it. Chdata identifies as this type, and names YouTuber PoisonedPotion as another. The ~2% here reflects how thin the explicit signal is in the data — but it almost certainly undercounts a latent population that would surface if the hunt-and-escape loop were ever given good mechanics.

🏟️ The Safe PvP Minigame Enjoyer~3%

Wants to PvP, but not through risk. Their home is the safe, structured minigame — LMS, the PvP Arena, Castle Wars, Soul Wars — where you fight other players without staking your bank on it. This is a fundamentally different axis from every other archetype here: where the others are defined by their relationship to Wilderness risk (tolerating it, resenting it, wanting it, or fleeing it), this player simply opts out of risk while still loving the player-versus-player combat itself. They're under-sampled in this dataset specifically because it's built from "wildy" and "pvp" searches — minigame-only players aren't posting in Wilderness threads — but the signal is real: there's a steady stream of comments about safe-minigame problems (LMS reward changes, the Arena's broken matchmaking and downtime, Castle Wars and Soul Wars balance), it's just quieter than the Wilderness discourse. And the underlying preference is widely shared — a lot of players who'd never touch the Wilderness still sympathize with simply not liking risk. Chdata is a die-hard example of this type, and the people filling those minigame lobbies evidently are too (though hard numbers are scarce). The ~3% is conservative; this is likely a much larger population than a wildy-centric dataset can see.

🎯 The PvP Loyalist / PKer~10%

Still actively PKs or closely identifies with PvP culture. Heavily represented in source 3 (Oda's audience). Internally split between legitimate PKers who want real fights with other PKers, and loot pinata hunters who primarily target PvMers and have no interest in fair fights. Sources 2-3 surfaced this split explicitly — several commenters called the latter group the primary reason PvP is dying.

🏰 The Ironman Bloc~2%

Estimated ~30% of the playerbase. Gets nothing from kills, loses hard-earned items on death, can't rebuy from GE. Zero incentive to fight back. The most politically powerful anti-PvP vote in polls. Consistently referenced across the sources as the demographic any PvP update must account for. Bond Coffer + Blessed Status together are the only proposals that speak directly to their situation.

🕰️ The Nostalgic Ex-PKer~1%

Emerged clearly in sources 2 and 3 — players who loved PvP in 2006-2012, quit when it became too sweaty or when EOC dropped, and now watch PvP content without playing it. "We just need to be 13 and garbage at the game again." Potentially persuadable back into at least casual engagement, but only if the skill ceiling is lowered — not by tutorials, but by mechanically simpler combat options. A distinct group from the Willing-but-Helpless because they know what PvP used to be and are grieving its loss rather than trying to enter a foreign system.

📺 The DMM Convert~1%

Identified for the first time in source 9 (new-sorted Reddit, June 2026) following DMM All Stars 3. A wave of players who were previously indifferent or hostile to PvP, converted by watching rather than playing. Dozens of posts with the same structure: "I've never touched LMS/Bounty Hunter/the Wilderness for PvP, but after watching DMM All Stars I want to try." Distinct from the Willing-but-Helpless (who already wanted to and were blocked) and the Nostalgic Ex-PKer (who used to play). These players had no pre-existing PvP interest and were pulled in purely by spectator experience — their existence is the strongest evidence in the dataset that PvP content that is watchable is a more effective onboarding funnel than any tutorial. The 2013 demographics data (source 9) shows PvP was once the #2 most popular activity at ~30% of players; the DMM Convert represents the population that could potentially restore some of that share. Mechanically, they want low-stakes structured entry — not the open Wilderness, but something with a defined format and stakes they control. Many explicitly mentioned trying LMS immediately after watching DMM, and finding it hostile.

🤐 Commented But Took No Position~18%

A genuine category, not a discard pile. These are people who engaged with PvP topics — jokes, memes, one-line reactions, tangents, "RIP," tagging a friend — but whose comments carry no extractable stance on the design questions. They took the time to show up under a PvP video or wilderness thread, which is itself a weak signal of interest, but you can't infer what they actually want. They're surfaced heavily in the meme-and-reaction layer of every source (especially the image/clip posts). Worth tracking because they represent latent, uncommitted attention: a chunk of the audience that is present for the conversation but hasn't been given a reason to land anywhere. From a persuasion standpoint they're the wildcard — neither won nor lost — and a clear, watchable case (see the DMM Convert) is the most plausible way to move some of them into a stance.

📝 Note from Chdata

I have a certain conclusion about some of the under-represented archetypes here. I consider all of these groups to be "fence-sitters" who'd probably engage in PvP more if it were designed better — not dead ends, but potential PvPers: a playerbase that could be grown rather than one that's simply absent. The groups I have in mind:

  • The "Indiana Jones" — already positive about predator-prey design, just starved of a good version of it.
  • The Willing But Helpless — want in, blocked by the skill cliff and risk.
  • The Nostalgic Ex-PKer — used to play, would come back if the barriers were lower.
  • The DMM Converts — pulled in by spectating, looking for a low-stakes way to actually try it.
  • The Ironman Bloc — locked out by how punishing loss is for them specifically.

These small slices aren't the ceiling on who could engage with a healthier Wilderness — they're the seeds of it. The whole argument of the video is that the right design could convert latent interest into real participation, and these are the groups where that upside lives.

There's a catch, though: Jagex only develops content based on what the community gives the biggest signal toward. So unless that philosophy changes — or the community decides these groups are worth exploring — Jagex is unlikely to design content meant to entice these types of players in the first place. The one bright spot is that there is some real community signaling to improve PvP for ironmen and for the "Willing But Helpless," so those two have a path that doesn't depend on the philosophy shifting.

All thirteen sources now paint a consistent picture. The full package is below the 70% threshold by a significant margin. The PvP Tutorial / Proving Grounds concept remains the single highest-enthusiasm idea — and it has now passed its lock-in poll, making it the one item here that's moved from "estimated approval" to confirmed content. Source 5 (TheoryWiser podcast) produced the clearest articulation of why it's needed: "Imagine if you wanted to complete the Inferno but there was nothing to properly train you beyond Fight Caves." That framing resonates across every archetype except the hardline anti-PvP bloc.

The ELO/matchmaking system — which Chdata does address (the existing PvP Arena is meant to have ranked matchmaking but underdelivers, and his proposed 6v6 PvP Arena builds in a matchmaking/ranking system) — continues to accumulate cross-source support. Source 5's chess grandmaster analogy ("Imagine learning chess by playing against grandmasters") drew 41 likes and 13 replies, one of the highest-engagement single comments in the dataset. Every successful competitive game uses skill-based matching; OSRS's absence of it is increasingly framed as a design oversight rather than an intentional choice. The newest data drives this home: source 13 (wildy new-sorted) surfaced a player who built and shipped a skill-based PvP matchmaking and leaderboard plugin for RuneLite — roughly 77,000 players rated across 250,000 matches — explicitly because the game won't do it itself. When the community is building the matchmaking system as a third-party plugin, the demand signal is hard to argue with. Worth explicit signal-boosting in the video.

Note on methodology: These are engagement-informed estimates, not real polls — nobody has voted on most of these. Each figure is built from the archetype voting model in Section 1: take the engagement-weighted share of each mindset (with the no-position slice removed, since non-voters don't decide polls), estimate how likely each would be to vote yes on that specific proposal, then sum across the voter base. A proposal that pleases the large Reluctant-Visitor and Willing-but-Helpless blocs scores higher than one that mainly serves PKers, who are a smaller share of the conversation. Reddit upvotes are tracked separately from YouTube likes and carry different signal weight — a high-upvote Reddit post reflects broad passive agreement, while a high-like YouTube comment reflects active engagement from a viewing audience. Sources skew toward engaged, PvP-aware players who chose to comment on PvP content; the actual polling electorate includes far more inactive accounts and bot votes that skew anti-PvP. The numbers were re-derived across all 13 sources and will shift as new data arrives. Treat them as upper-bound, directional estimates relative to the 70% poll threshold, not predictions of an exact poll result — real polls also swing on question wording, JMod framing, and turnout, none of which this captures.

Community Rebuttals & Pushback
Arguments denying, dismissing, or countering the problems above · updated across 13 sources
Dismissals of Core Problems
  • "Just don't go in the Wilderness." The most common rebuttal across all sources. Ignores forced interaction via clue steps, MA2 capes, Voidwaker, diary rewards. Strongest in source 3 (Still Framed's wilderness video) where the audience skewed heavily toward non-PvP players.
  • "You chose to play Ironman. Stop asking for changes to the core game." Ignores that ironmen are ~30% of the playerbase and their voting bloc has outsized poll influence. Ironmen largely agree they shouldn't be forced into the Wilderness at all — they want the items moved, not the risk reduced.
  • "Just learn to anti-PK. PvP has been in the game for 20 years." Dominant rebuttal in sources 2-3. Source 5 produced the most precise counter — the Inferno analogy: nobody would learn the Inferno if there was no training between Fight Caves and the Inferno itself. Dismissing the learning gap doesn't make it disappear.
  • "The Wilderness should be risky — that's the entire point." Mischaracterizes structural proposals as removing risk. Blessed Status doesn't remove risk — it redirects what is risked. Source 5 sharpened the rebuttal: the argument collapses when you distinguish between risk-as-design-tool and risk-as-griefing-vector.
  • "LMS / BH / Castle Wars already exists. Just use those." Appeared heavily in sources 2-3 and 5. Ignores that LMS is bot-infested, BH has been through multiple death-and-rebirth cycles, and Castle Wars has been dead for years. The argument proves the problem.
  • "PvP updates attract gold farmers and RWT. Jagex is right to deprioritize it." Genuine integrity concern with cross-archetype credibility. Source 5 added nuance: commenters noted that bot farms are nuking the value of wilderness items regardless of PvP updates, suggesting the bot problem and the PvP problem are separable.
  • "PvP is a niche and will never be mainstream. Stop wasting dev time." Fatalism rebuttal that appeared strongly in source 5. Not angry, just resigned. Some of the most analytically rigorous comments in the dataset took this position — hard to dismiss as ignorance.
  • "Juicing drop tables just creates better loot piñatas." New from source 5 — the most pointed structural critique of the standard "fix" the PvP community usually asks for. The argument: if you make wilderness content more rewarding, you just give PKers better victims, not better fights. Directly challenges the drop-table-boost approach that dominates PvP lobbying.
  • "The sweats and streamers killed it. No update fixes that." Sources 2-3 and 5. Pins the problem on player behavior rather than design. Partially correct — the Nostalgic Ex-PKer archetype specifically cites the meta getting too sweaty as their exit reason — but ignores that design choices created the conditions for that meta to dominate.

Rebuttals to Specific Proposals
  • Against opt-in PvP toggle: "That just makes the Wilderness RS3." Largely correct — a full opt-out breaks the ecosystem. No serious proposal suggests this but it gets raised as a strawman whenever anyone asks for less forced interaction.
  • Against PvP tutorials / wave minigames: "NPCs won't teach you tick-perfect prayer switches — you just have to get destroyed by real players." Most-debated specific rebuttal in sources 2-3 and 5. Partially valid. Source 5 added a nuance: the tutorial doesn't need to make you competitive against top-level players, it just needs to lower the floor enough that the Willing-but-Helpless can get started without losing their bank.
  • Against ELO / matchmaking: "Bots will farm low-rank brackets and make it worthless." Legitimate concern given LMS's bot history. Source 5 commenters noted Emir's Arena already has elo but it's been ruined by players quitting early for imbue scroll rewards — the reward structure poisons the competitive intent.
  • Against limiting world count: "That just concentrates PKers onto the PvMers who remain." PvMers fear this specifically. Requires simultaneous PKer-vs-PKer incentives.
  • Against alternative sources for Wilderness items: "If Voidwaker leaves the Wilderness, why would anyone go there?" Genuine ecosystem tension. Source 5 noted the voidwaker passed polls with 85% approval as recently as 2023 — a signal that the community itself hasn't consistently opposed wilderness exclusives.
  • Against any development time on PvP at all: "Sailing / new raids first." Cross-source political argument. Source 5 added a pointed version: 90% of the modern playerbase has no interest in PvP, so catering to the 10% is a poor resource allocation. The Priority Ranking Poll system is the direct answer.

Hardline Positions (Lost Votes — Argue Around, Not At)
  • "Remove PvP from the Wilderness entirely." Identity-level opposition consistent across all eight sources at roughly the same frequency. Source 5 gave this position its most thoughtful expression — not spite-voting, but a reasoned argument that PvP's core mechanic (theft) is incompatible with a game where people pay real money for in-game progression. Don't engage; argue around them.
  • "I'll vote no on every PvP poll forever out of spite." Explicitly stated in all eight sources. Acknowledge in the video and move on. Arguing with it gives it oxygen.
  • "PvP is already dead and that's fine with me." Fatalistic but not hostile. Strongest in sources 3 and 5. Several source 5 commenters took this position from a structural analysis standpoint rather than spite — that's actually harder to rebut than simple spite.
  • "The wilderness is fundamentally theft and I won't participate in or support it." New in source 5. Framed as a moral/economic argument, not a gameplay complaint. Not reachable by any design change.

Opt-in overhead costing 100k. Items kept on death for a 5% fee paid to the PKer. Teams must be fully Blessed or not at all. PKers don't risk gear fighting Blessed players.

Likely Yes

Reluctant Visitors and Willing-but-Helpless who want some death protection. PvM Enjoyers who'd enter the Wilderness more if the downside was capped. PvP Loyalists are deeply divided. Legitimate PKers who want real fights see potential value in more willing targets. Loot-pinata hunters specifically lose access to RNG item pulls and may oppose it. Sources 2-3 confirmed this internal split is real — the loot pinata vs. real PKer divide is not a framing device, it's a genuine faction difference.

Likely No

Anti-PvP Bloc who don't care about any Wilderness update. Hardcore PKers who read the 5% fee as "not enough" compared to current gambling on carry value. A small contingent who argue Blessed Status lets bots PvM in the Wilderness with reduced consequence.

Key framing insight from multiple sources: The pitch to PKers should emphasize guaranteed income over RNG gambling. Killing a Blessed player is a reliable paycheck. Killing a random PvMer is a lottery ticket. Sources 2-3 showed many PKers don't frame it this way on their own — the video needs to make this explicit.

Official small-group party system with anti-friendly-fire, loot split mechanics, and a recruitment board at Ferox Enclave.

Likely Yes

"I'd PvP if I could do it with friends" was a recurring sentiment across the sources. The Willing-but-Helpless specifically cite the social gap — no easy way to find or coordinate with other learners. The recruitment board component appeals to solo players who want group content without needing an existing clan.

Split

Existing PvP clans who already organize through Discord don't see a need for it, but few actively oppose it. The anti-friendly-fire mechanic is the most contentious element — sources 2-3 showed a few comments reading it as "dumbing down" multi combat.

Likely No

Very few explicit objections across any source. Passive opposition (just not caring) rather than active. This is the safest proposal in the package.

Framing note: Source 3 added new context — the lone-wolf-vs-zerg experience came up repeatedly. Frame Wildy Teams as the counter to being 20-manned, not just as a social convenience feature. That framing connects to Team Combat Zones and makes the two proposals feel like a coherent system rather than separate asks.
🏟️ Team Combat Zones

Zone type where any one group faces at most 6 attackers at a time. Structured multi combat rather than unrestricted pile-ons.

Source 10 validation: "Just delete Multi from Wildy" (4,413 upvotes, 879 comments) is the highest-upvoted directly relevant post in the wildy-search dataset and independently restates the TCZ design principle: "No one is scared of 1 pker. The community is converging on this design space without knowing about TCZ specifically.
Likely Yes

Anyone who has been 20-manned in multi doing boss content. Small-group PvPers who want fair structured fights. PvMers who want to do Wilderness bosses without a 30-man clan camping the entrance. Sources 2-3 showed the "pkers only hit pvmers and log when another pker logs in" complaint was widespread — TCZ is a structural answer to that.

Split

Mid-sized clans (10-15 players) who aren't running zerg tactics but would be directly affected by the cap. Some read this as Jagex picking winners in the multi meta. Source 3 showed clan-oriented players specifically skeptical of anything that restricts their group size.

Likely No

Large clan leadership who rely on overwhelming numbers as their core tactic. This is the proposal with the most organized potential opposition. Notably, sources 2-3 didn't increase the "likely yes" — TCZ remains a hard sell because the people it helps aren't writing in to say so, while the people it hurts are.

Persuasion challenge: The people TCZ most helps — casual multi-PvMers and small-group fighters — are the least likely to be watching PvP YouTube content and voting in PvP polls. This is a structural problem with the proposal's political path, not a flaw in the design itself. Explicitly calling this out in the video might be the most honest move.

Community ranks potential PvP updates by tier. Jagex commits to developing the top-ranked one next cycle.

Anyone frustrated with the broken polling system — which is nearly everyone across the sources, PvP or not. Gives all player types agency without requiring agreement on one specific update. Source 5 produced several long, thoughtful comments from this group specifically — they remember what PvP felt like when it was fun and are grieving that, not fundamentally opposed. Mechanically simpler engagement options (Blessed Status reducing stakes, Wildy Teams providing context for fights to happen) speak to them more than tutorials.
  • Ironmen who do Wilderness PvM — the "chaos altar is great but getting PK'd is brutal" crowd. Bond Coffer and Blessed Status together address their actual situation.   • PvMers who've been zerged in multi — Team Combat Zones solves their specific lived experience.   • Legitimate PKers who want real fights — sources 2-3 and 5 consistently surfaced this faction. Source 5 specifically had comments from active PKers frustrated that the loot-pinata hunters dominate PvP's public image and poison every poll. They're natural allies for ELO matching, Wildy Teams, and TCZ. One source 5 commenter suggested clan-focused PvP with actual objectives — worth noting as signal that even within the PKer community, there's appetite for designed PvP over unstructured griefing.
  • Players despairing about the poll system — widespread across all eight sources. Source 5 sharpened this: several commenters articulated that the polling system is structurally incapable of ever passing meaningful PvP content as long as non-PvPers are included in the vote. The Priority Ranking Poll system is the direct structural answer — but it needs to be pitched as changing the process, not just asking nicer.

✖ Hard or Impossible to Convince
  • "Remove PvP entirely" contingent — identity-level opposition consistent across all eight sources. Several source 5 comments took this position from a reasoned structural analysis rather than spite, making it harder to dismiss. No proposal engages with this. Argue around them.
  • Spite voters — explicitly stated across all eight sources. Source 5 added a specific flavour: commenters who were camped at Mage Arena, or killed for monk robes at Calvarion, and made a permanent decision to vote no on all PvP forever. These are lost votes regardless of proposal quality. Acknowledge them and move on.
  • Large clan players who rely on zerg mechanics — TCZ directly curtails their playstyle. Unchanged across eight sources.
  • Loot-pinata-only PKers — the faction who specifically want to hunt non-PvP players and have no interest in fair fights. Sources 2-3 and 5 confirmed they're a real group. Blessed Status reduces access to their preferred targets. Won't be turned.
  • Deep structural skeptics — new in source 5. Commenters who accept that PvP is declining, have analyzed why (skill gap death spiral, parasitic reward structure, aging playerbase), and have concluded that no design change within the current framework can fix it. These are not emotional arguments — they're the hardest to rebut because several of them are analytically sound. The best response is Chdata's portfolio itself: demonstrating that none of these specific proposals have actually been tried.
